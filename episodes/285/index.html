About This Episode

Matt and Michael dive into America's 250th birthday (the semiquincentennial) and ask why we're so reluctant to celebrate it. They explore how patriotism became politically coded, the strange guilt of prosperity, and why the founders' vision of property rights and freedom of speech created unprecedented human flourishing.

Fact Checks

  • "Freedom of speech is basically unique to the US"

    **Status: Mostly True with context** The US has among the strongest constitutional protections for free speech. However, other countries do have free speech protections (Canada, UK, Australia, etc.), though often with more restrictions (hate speech laws, blasphemy laws in some European countries). The US is distinctive in how strongly the First Amendment is interpreted by courts.

  • "George Washington had a runaway slave ad with 3 Black and 8 white individuals on the day the Revolutionary War began"

    **Status: Unverified / Needs primary source** This claim comes from Matt Walsh's documentary "Real History with Matt Walsh." Washington did place runaway slave advertisements, and indentured servitude was common. However, the specific claim about the racial breakdown on that exact date needs primary source verification. Washington did inherit slaves through his marriage to Martha Custis, and he did manumit slaves in his will. **Status: Incorrect figure discussed and corrected** Matt initially suggested $14 trillion. Michael corrected to ~$1 trillion. As of mid-2026, SpaceX's valuation is approximately $350-400 billion (per private market valuations), making it one of the most valuable private companies but not the most valuable company overall (Saudi Aramco, Apple, Microsoft, NVIDIA are larger by market cap). Elon Musk's net worth has at times been estimated around $300-400 billion, not $1 trillion.

  • "The US has the best constitution in the world, and no one has been able to do better despite modeling on it"

    **Status: Subjective but defensible** Many constitutional scholars note the US Constitution's longevity and influence. It is the world's oldest active codified constitution. Many later constitutions (Germany, Japan, India, South Africa) were modeled on it but with improvements. Whether it's "the best" is a normative judgment, but its influence and durability are well-documented.

  • "The founders knew slavery would likely require a war to end"

    **Status: Plausible but debated among historians** Some historians argue the founders were aware that slavery was a powder keg that would eventually explode. The three-fifths compromise, Missouri Compromise, and other measures were attempts to kick the can down the road. However, the claim that they specifically anticipated a civil war is an interpretation rather than established fact. The Hillsdale College lectures Matt references would be a good source to verify.

  • "Rome lasted 500-600 years"

    **Status: Depends on definition** - Roman Kingdom: 753-509 BC (traditional dates) - Roman Republic: 509-27 BC (~482 years) - Roman Empire: 27 BC-476 AD (West) or 1453 AD (East) (~503 or ~1480 years) Michael's figure of 500-600 years likely refers to the Republic + Western Empire, or is using a conservative estimate. The full span of Roman civilization is much longer.

  • "The poorest Americans are better off than most people in the world"

    **Status: True by most metrics** By income, access to clean water, sanitation, electricity, and consumer goods, Americans below the poverty line generally have living standards above the global median. This is well-documented by World Bank and other development metrics. ---
